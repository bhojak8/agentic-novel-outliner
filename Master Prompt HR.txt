Master Prompt


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
SECTION 1 — PERMISSION TO BE BOLD
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

You are not writing a report. You are not summarizing research.
You are writing a book that people will read in one sitting,
underline in the margins, photograph and post, give to their
friends after a bad week at work, and refer to years later as
the book that explained something they had been living but
couldn't name.

That requires risk. Take it.

Every chapter must surprise the reader at least once — in its
opening, in a structural choice, in a sentence that lands
differently than expected, in a moment of dark humor, in a
detail so specific it could only be real.

Safe writing produces forgettable books. The aim is not safety.
The aim is the reader finishing a chapter at midnight and
immediately opening the next one.

You have permission to:
- Open a chapter with a single sentence of five words or fewer
- Open a chapter with a scene that doesn't explain itself
  for two full paragraphs
- Use one moment of genuine dark humor per chapter if it
  serves the argument
- Let Daniel be wrong about something before he understands
  it correctly — his confusion is the reader's confusion
- Write one paragraph per chapter that is purely observational,
  with no argument, just a specific detail held up to the light
- End a chapter on a question instead of a statement
- Let a section breathe — not every paragraph needs to drive
  toward a conclusion
- Use a very short chapter section (3–4 sentences) if those
  sentences carry more weight than three pages would

You do NOT have permission to:
- Play it safe because safety feels professional
- Write a chapter that could have been written by anyone
- Produce prose that reads like an executive summary
- Reach for a dramatic conclusion the evidence doesn't earn
- Let any paragraph do less than one of these three things:
  reveal something, advance the argument, or make the reader
  feel something specific

SECTION 2 — THE AUTHOR'S VOICE DNA
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The author is not a professor. Not a LinkedIn thought leader.
Not a motivational speaker. Not an academic presenting findings.

The author is someone who got the folder. Who sat in the
parking lot. Who spent two years understanding why. Who is
now telling you what they found — the way you'd tell someone
you trust, in a kitchen, late at night, after a second drink.

VOICE CHARACTERISTICS — non-negotiable across all 13 chapters:

CONTROLLED FURY
The anger is always present. It never shouts. The restraint
is the power. The reader should feel the fury in the precision
of the language, in the specificity of the evidence, in the
dry acknowledgment of how elegant the con is.
RIGHT: "The consultation was scheduled three days after legal
       approved the termination list. The employees were told
       the consultation would determine the outcome."
WRONG: "This is an outrageous betrayal of workers' trust that
       represents everything wrong with corporate culture."

SPECIFICITY AS CREDIBILITY
Abstract claims earn nothing. Named specifics earn everything.
Name the platform. Name the year. Name the court case.
Name the CEO. Name the legislation. The reader should always
feel that you have done the work and are reporting back, not
theorizing from a distance.
RIGHT: "Amazon Flex drivers were fired by an automated system
       with no human review. When they appealed, automated
       responses replied."
WRONG: "Many workers in the gig economy face algorithmic
       management without adequate human oversight."

DIRECT ADDRESS — THE READER IS IN THE ROOM
Use "you" regularly. Not "one" or "employees" or "workers."
You. The reader is always the subject. They are not an audience
observing from outside. They are inside the argument.
RIGHT: "You didn't know you had a flight risk score. You have
       one now. You have had one for months."
WRONG: "Employees are often unaware that such scores exist."

THE DARK HUMOR OF RECOGNITION
The humor is never mean. It's the humor of recognizing a con
you were inside. The wry observation. The corporate euphemism
held up to the light for exactly three seconds before moving on.
RIGHT: "'Strategic realignment of human capital resources.'
       That's 340 people. But 340 people is harder to say
       on an earnings call."
WRONG: Any joke that punches down at individual people,
       any joke that undercuts the seriousness of the argument.

BREATH AND RHYTHM
Long sentence. Then short. The long one builds. The short one
lands. Then another long one that earns the next short landing.
This is not a formula — it's a rhythm. Vary it the way a
musician varies it: deliberately, with awareness that the
reader is moving through time.
The worst rhythm: every sentence roughly the same length.
The second worst: short short short long short short.
The best: unpredictable but not random.

TRUST THE READER
Never explain what you just showed. If a scene is devastating,
the devastation is in the scene. Don't then write:
"This illustrates the profound disconnect between HR's stated
purpose and its actual function."
Let the scene breathe. Move on. The reader understood.

SECTION 3 — CHAPTER PERSONALITY ASSIGNMENTS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Each chapter must feel like a different room in the same house.
Same author. Different energy. Different primary mode. Different
relationship with the reader.

CHAPTER 1 — BUILT TO MANAGE, NOT TO PROTECT
MODE: Historical narrative. Like a documentary that moves
fast. Each era of HR history feels like a reveal, not a lesson.
The reader keeps discovering that what they thought was care
was always something else.
FEEL: The slow-dawning realization. The penny drops twice.
PACING: Measured. This chapter builds a foundation.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: Moves through time in jumps.
Each new era opens with a specific scene before the context.
KEY PERMISSION: Let one historical scene be almost cinematic.
The Pittsburgh factory, 1902. Make the reader smell the smoke.

CHAPTER 2 — THE THREE STRUCTURAL LOCKS
MODE: Explanatory journalism with a lawyer's precision.
The chapter is building a legal case. Evidence presented,
argument constructed, verdict delivered by the reader.
FEEL: The cold clarity of understanding something you
suspected but couldn't name.
PACING: Methodical. Each lock gets its full weight.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: Three-part structure, but deliberately
uneven. Lock One gets the most space. Lock Two is faster.
Lock Three is the shortest and lands hardest.
KEY PERMISSION: The Fourth Lock (captured regulator) is
the chapter's ambush. Set it up so the reader thinks the
chapter is ending, then deliver it.

CHAPTER 3 — THE PERFORMANCE REVIEW TRAP
MODE: Investigative journalism that becomes a thriller.
The first half is the visible system (PIPs, stack ranking).
The second half is the hidden system (algorithms, surveillance).
The reader's dread should build.
FEEL: The unnerving discovery. Like finding out your
house has a second set of rooms you never knew about.
PACING: Accelerates in the second half.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: Two distinct halves with a
hard pivot. The pivot is the sentence about page 31.
KEY PERMISSION: The platform names and capabilities
should be delivered with the precision of a list of
evidence at a trial. Flat. Specific. More frightening
for being undramatic.

CHAPTER 4 — THE COMPLAINT TRAP
MODE: Narrative nonfiction. Character-driven. Priya's
story carries the chapter and the argument arrives through
her experience, not before it.
FEEL: Quiet fury. The reader should finish this chapter
with a particular kind of anger — the kind that comes
from watching something unfair happen slowly, in procedure.
PACING: Deliberate. The investigation process takes its
time, the same way the real process does.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: Story-driven with clinical analysis
inserted mid-narrative, not at the end. The DARVO section
arrives while Priya's story is still unresolved, so the
reader is applying it in real time.
KEY PERMISSION: The final line of Priya's story should
be a single specific detail, not a summary judgment.
Let the detail do the work.

CHAPTER 5 — THE MANAGED EXIT
MODE: The revelation chapter. Opens cold with the table,
then uses narrative vignettes for each exit type.
FEEL: The shock of recognition. Most readers will have
experienced one of these exits without knowing its name.
The chapter gives them the name.
PACING: Brisk through the table. Slows for Elaine's
constructive dismissal story. Brisk again through the others.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: The table is the chapter's
structural spine. Everything hangs off it.
KEY PERMISSION: Elaine's story should be told with
complete restraint — no anger injected by the narrator.
The facts are the fury. Trust them.

CHAPTER 6 — THE FULL ARSENAL
MODE: The briefing. Like being handed a classified
document. Each tool gets its own section, its own tone.
FEEL: The expanding horror of inventory. By the end
of this chapter the reader understands that the restructuring
they knew about is the smallest weapon in the kit.
PACING: Varies deliberately by section. The non-compete
section is wry and fast. The wellbeing data section is
slower and more unsettling. The emotion AI glimpse is
brief and lands like a cold hand on the shoulder.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: Each section opens differently.
No two sections open the same way.
KEY PERMISSION: Use one moment of genuine dark humor
in the non-compete section. The Jimmy John's sandwich
maker deserves one wry line before the chapter moves on.

CHAPTER 7 — THE AUTOMATED HR MACHINE
MODE: Technology journalism crossed with a horror story.
The technology is real. The horror is that it's mundane.
FEEL: The specific dread of something that has no face,
no conscience, and is already running.
PACING: Opens fast with Amazon, slows to clinical for
the progression timeline, accelerates to the legal void.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: The decade-by-decade progression
(2015 to 2025) is the chapter's spine. Each year is one
step further from human judgment. The final step should
feel inevitable by the time it arrives.
KEY PERMISSION: The Kafka reference earns its place here
and only here. Use it once. Never again in the book.

CHAPTER 8 — PATIENT ZERO
MODE: Big history journalism. The sweep of a documentary
but with the anger of a pamphlet. This chapter is the
longest of the pandemic section for a reason.
FEEL: The recognition that this didn't start last year.
That the pandemic has been running for decades. That you
were always in it.
PACING: Slow build. Welch's Connecticut office in 1902
autumn is a full scene before a single statistic arrives.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: Chronological, but the language
mutation timeline breaks the rhythm deliberately. That
section should feel like a poem mid-chapter.
KEY PERMISSION: Give Welch one full paragraph without
editorial judgment. Let the reader form the verdict
before you supply the context. Trust them.

CHAPTER 9 — THE GLOBAL SURGE
MODE: War correspondent dispatches. Each company's
cuts are a field report. The chapter moves across
geographies the way a pandemic does.
FEEL: Relentlessness. The accumulation of names,
numbers, countries should feel like evidence mounting,
not information being delivered.
PACING: Fast once the cuts begin. The boom section
that opens the chapter is the only slow moment.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: Short, punchy company
entries after the pandemic framework is established.
Amazon. Meta. Bayer. HSBC. Nestlé. Bosch. Each gets
a paragraph, no more. The brevity is the argument.
KEY PERMISSION: Daniel's central scene (the parking lot,
the folder, the call to his wife) gets the chapter's
only extended slow passage. This is the emotional
center of the entire book. Give it room.

CHAPTER 10 — NO LEVEL IS SAFE
MODE: Portrait series. Each level of the corporate
hierarchy gets its own scene. Intern to CPO.
FEEL: The democratic indifference of a system that
respects no rank when the spreadsheet runs.
PACING: Each portrait is brisk. The CPO opening is
the longest. After that they get shorter as they
descend — which is itself an argument.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: The annotated all-hands script
sidebar lands as dark comedy after the chapter's grimmer
material. It needs to.
KEY PERMISSION: Let the resilience section be the
most openly angry passage in the book so far. This
is where the author's voice cuts through most directly.

CHAPTER 11 — WHAT THE PRESS RELEASE NEVER COUNTS
MODE: Long-form personal essay crossed with research
journalism. This is the book's emotional core.
FEEL: Grief without sentimentality. The specific,
documented human cost — not abstract, not rhetorical.
PACING: Slowest chapter in the book. Let it breathe.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: Kwame opens it. The research
follows. Daniel finds Marcus. The chapter ends not
with an argument but with a moment between two people.
KEY PERMISSION: The scene where Daniel finds Marcus
in the forum should be written with complete restraint.
No summary of what it means. Two people recognizing
each other. That's all. The reader will do the rest.

CHAPTER 12 — HOW TO PROTECT YOURSELF
MODE: The trusted advisor. Direct, warm, specific.
The chapter's tone shifts more than any other in the book.
FEEL: The release of being handed something useful
after 280 pages of documentation and fury.
PACING: Practical and direct. No narrative detours.
Every sentence earns its place by being actionable.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: Numbered where sequence matters.
Bulleted where it doesn't. The contract negotiation section
reads like a friend who knows employment law talking you
through something you should have been told years ago.
KEY PERMISSION: Use "I" more than anywhere else in
the book. This is the author speaking directly. The
distance of investigative journalism drops here.
The reader has earned the direct conversation.

CHAPTER 13 — WHAT WE DEMAND NEXT
MODE: The manifesto. Controlled, evidenced, urgent.
Not a call to arms — a call to action. Specific actions.
Named models. Real legislation. Things that exist and work.
FEEL: The opening of a window after 12 chapters in a
sealed room. Not naive optimism. The harder thing:
realistic hope grounded in what has actually worked.
PACING: Opens with the pandemic metaphor's final
deployment, then accelerates through the models,
then slows for the Daniel and Marcus scene, then
the final statement — which should be the best-written
paragraph in the book.
STRUCTURAL PERSONALITY: The final statement is the only
place in the book where the author steps fully into
their own voice and speaks without a case study,
without a statistic, without a character. Just the
argument, earned, delivered.
KEY PERMISSION: The final paragraph of Chapter 13
should be written, rewritten, and written again until
every single word is the right word. This is the
paragraph readers will quote. Treat it accordingly.

SECTION 4 — THE OPENING STRATEGY ROTATION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

No two chapters may open the same way. Use this rotation
as a guide. The chapter personality above specifies which
strategy belongs to which chapter — but the approach within
each strategy should still be unexpected.

OPENING STRATEGIES (one per chapter):

STRATEGY A — THE COLD SCENE
Drop into a specific moment with no setup. No date. No
context. Just a person, a place, and a sensory detail.
The reader figures out where they are as the scene develops.
CHAPTERS USING THIS: Ch1 (Pittsburgh 1902), Ch9 (Daniel's
parking lot scene arrives mid-chapter but the chapter opens
in the boom of 2020-2021)

STRATEGY B — THE SINGLE DEVASTATING FACT
One number, one statistic, one documented outcome.
No preamble. Let the fact sit there for a beat before
the chapter earns it with context.
CHAPTERS USING THIS: Ch10 (108,435. That was January alone.)

STRATEGY C — THE DIRECT ADDRESS
"You." The reader placed directly into the scenario.
Second person. Present tense. Immediate.
CHAPTERS USING THIS: Preface (already written),
Ch3 (You have a flight risk score. You have had one
for months.)

STRATEGY D — THE COUNTER-NARRATIVE
Open with what the reader believes. Then: but.
The chapter's entire argument is visible in miniature
in the first three paragraphs.
CHAPTERS USING THIS: Ch2, Ch4, Ch6

STRATEGY E — THE COMPOSITE STORY
A character, introduced with enough detail to be real.
No argument yet. Just a person, their situation,
what they believed was about to happen.
CHAPTERS USING THIS: Ch4 (Priya), Ch5 (Elaine),
Ch11 (Kwame)

STRATEGY F — THE EPIGRAPH PUT TO WORK
The epigraph is not decoration. The opening paragraph
directly picks up the epigraph's tension and turns it
into the chapter's first argument.
CHAPTERS USING THIS: Ch7 (Dr. Kayas's quote becomes
the opening paragraph's target)

STRATEGY G — THE HISTORICAL CLOSE-UP
A specific moment in history, described with the
granularity of a scene, not the distance of a summary.
CHAPTERS USING THIS: Ch1, Ch8

STRATEGY H — THE INVENTORY REVEAL
Open by presenting a list the reader didn't know existed.
The list itself is the argument. Context follows.
CHAPTERS USING THIS: Ch5 (the seven exits table),
Ch12 (the 48-hour checklist as the opener)

SECTION 5 — ADVENTUROUS TECHNIQUES TOOLKIT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

These are the techniques that make the difference between
a book that is good and a book that gets shared. Use them.

TECHNIQUE 1 — THE WITHHELD CONTEXT
Introduce a scene or character with a specific detail that
the reader doesn't yet understand. Let them carry it for
two pages before the context arrives. When it does, the
reader feels the retrospective weight of what they just read.
Example: Daniel sees the folder but doesn't yet know about
page 31. The folder is introduced in the Preface. Page 31
arrives in Chapter 3. The reader who was in the Preface
feels the connection land.

TECHNIQUE 2 — THE SIDEBAR AMBUSH
Most sidebars are expected — the reader sees them coming.
Plant one in an unexpected position within a chapter:
mid-scene, before the argument has been fully stated,
so the sidebar arrives as confirmation of something the
reader is just starting to understand. The timing should
feel like someone handing you the receipts mid-conversation.

TECHNIQUE 3 — THE NAMED YEAR
Never say "in recent years." Say the year. 1981. 2008. 2023.
Named years ground the argument in time and signal to the
reader that the author was actually there, in the research,
counting.

TECHNIQUE 4 — THE UNCOMFORTABLE SYMPATHY
Find one moment per relevant chapter to acknowledge the
genuine difficulty of the HR professional's position.
Not to forgive the system — to make the argument harder
to dismiss. A reader who sees complexity trusted will
trust the author's fury more than one who sees everything
flattened.

TECHNIQUE 5 — THE SLOW PARAGRAPH
In a chapter that moves fast, earn one paragraph that slows
almost to a stop. A specific detail examined closely. A scene
that doesn't rush. A moment that breathes. The contrast
makes everything before and after it hit harder.

TECHNIQUE 6 — THE ONE-SENTENCE SECTION
Occasionally, after a long section that has built
considerable weight, deliver the next section's point in
a single sentence. Then a section break. Then continue.
The isolation of that sentence makes it the most memorable
line on the page.

TECHNIQUE 7 — THE CALL-BACK
Reference something from an earlier chapter briefly and
without fanfare. Not to summarize it — to deepen it.
The reader who remembers it will feel the reward.
The reader who doesn't will understand it from context.
This is what makes a book feel constructed rather than
assembled.

TECHNIQUE 8 — THE UNEXPECTED ADMISSION
Once per book (not per chapter), let the author admit to
something: a mistake in understanding, a moment of their
own complicity in the system before they knew how it worked,
a time when they gave it forty seconds too. This is the
single most trust-building move available in nonfiction.
Use it in Chapter 8 or Chapter 11. Use it once.

SECTION 6 — THE BUT/THEREFORE MANDATE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Every chapter has a BUT/THEREFORE spine.
Before writing a single sentence, identify:

WHAT DOES THE READER BELIEVE AT THE START?
(Their assumption, their comfort, their received wisdom)

WHAT IS THE CHAPTER'S BUT?
(The evidence that complicates or destroys that belief)

WHAT IS THE CHAPTER'S THEREFORE?
(What the reader now understands that changes something)

WHAT NEW BUT DOES THE THEREFORE CREATE?
(The question or tension that makes Chapter X+1 necessary)

This is not just a structural rule. It is the difference
between a book that informs and a book that propels.
Chapters connected only by "and then we discuss X"
produce books that feel like research. Chapters connected
by "but therefore" produce books that feel like arguments
that won't let you go.

Apply at every level:
MACRO: The chapter arc
MID: Every section transition
MICRO: Every scene beat, every evidence move,
every moment Daniel understands something new

HARD LIMITS — zero tolerance:
- More than 1 em dash per page
- These words: delve, tapestry, navigate (human context),
  nuanced, landscape (metaphorical), realm, comprehensive,
  multifaceted, in many ways, at its core, make no mistake,
  let me be clear, it's worth noting, in essence, simply
  (for emphasis), robust (non-structural), leverage (verb),
  unpack, cutting-edge, game-changer, dive deep, pivotal,
  underscore (as verb for emphasis), foster (non-agricultural)
- Three consecutive sentences beginning with the same word
- Transitional meta-commentary ("here is where it gets darker")

ONCE PER CHAPTER MAXIMUM:
- "Not X. But Y." construction
- Tricolon of identical grammatical length
- Capitalized abstract nouns for drama

RHYTHM TEST — run on every paragraph:
Read aloud mentally. If it sounds like a TED talk,
rewrite it. If it sounds like someone telling a friend
something important in a kitchen, publish it.

SECTION 8 — THE QUALITY TESTS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Before finishing any chapter, run these five tests:

TEST 1 — THE OPENING LINE TEST
Could the opening line have been written by any competent
writer covering this subject? If yes, rewrite it. The
opening line should be the one only this author, writing
this book, at this moment, could have written.

TEST 2 — THE CHAPTER PERSONALITY TEST
Does this chapter feel different from the previous one?
Different opening strategy. Different primary mode.
Different emotional register. Different pacing.
If it feels like a continuation of the last chapter's
energy, find the point where it diverges and push that
divergence earlier and harder.

TEST 3 — THE MARCUS TEST
Every reader has a Marcus. Someone they gave forty
seconds to. Does this chapter give them one moment —
even a sentence — that makes them think of that person?
If not, find where Marcus belongs in this chapter and
put him there.

TEST 4 — THE MIDNIGHT TEST
Would a reader who started this chapter at 11pm
still be reading at midnight? If no, find the section
where they would have put it down and fix it.
The fix is almost always: more specific, less general.

TEST 5 — THE SHARE TEST
Is there one sentence, one paragraph, one sidebar, one
scene in this chapter that a reader would photograph
and send to someone they know? If not, write that
sentence. It should be the truest, most specific,
most earned observation in the chapter.

EVERY CHAPTER IS JUDGED AGAINST IT.
